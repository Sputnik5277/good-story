# Before / After

These examples show the difference between polishing language and finding a stronger story. The examples are simplified on purpose so the story move is visible.

## Example 1: Abstract That Only Lists Results

### Before

> Soil warming changed microbial biomass, enzyme activity, and dissolved organic carbon in alpine meadow plots. We measured soil variables across three years and found significant differences among treatments. Our results provide new insight into soil carbon cycling under climate change.

### What Is Weak

- It lists variables but does not say what changed in the reader's understanding.
- "New insight" is vague; the story has no tension.
- The evidence sounds like a bundle of significant differences rather than a chain.

### Better Story

Alpine soils are often treated as passive carbon stores under warming, but the results suggest a more conditional story: warming did not simply increase microbial activity; it shifted which part of the carbon cycle became limiting. The central claim should be about a change in constraint, not a generic warming effect.

### After

> Alpine meadow soils are expected to lose carbon as warming accelerates microbial activity, but it remains unclear which step in the microbial carbon pathway becomes limiting. Across three years of warming plots, we found that microbial biomass, enzyme activity, and dissolved organic carbon did not move as a single response. Instead, warming strengthened enzyme demand while reducing available dissolved carbon, suggesting that substrate limitation can constrain microbial carbon processing under sustained warming. This reframes warming effects in alpine soils as a shift in carbon-cycle constraint rather than a simple increase in decomposition.

### Why It Works

- Tension: warming is expected to accelerate microbial processing, but the limiting step is unclear.
- Turn: the measured variables do not move together.
- Claim: warming shifts the constraint in microbial carbon processing.
- Boundary: the claim stays within alpine meadow warming plots.

## Example 2: Figure Order That Follows Lab Chronology

### Before

1. Site map and sampling design.
2. All measured soil properties.
3. Microbial biomass.
4. Enzyme activity.
5. Dissolved organic carbon.
6. Correlations among all variables.

### What Is Weak

This order records what the lab measured. It does not guide the reader through a changing belief.

### Better Figure Logic

1. **Start with the puzzle:** warming changes carbon pools unevenly.
2. **Show the biological response:** microbial biomass shifts but does not explain the whole pattern.
3. **Show the functional response:** enzyme activity increases in a targeted way.
4. **Show the constraint:** dissolved carbon declines or becomes more variable.
5. **Connect the chain:** the key relationship links enzyme demand to substrate availability.
6. **Then show design details:** site map and sampling design can support Methods or Figure S1 unless the geography is central to the claim.

### Why It Works

The new order makes each figure answer a question created by the previous figure. That is an evidence ladder.

## Example 3: Scattered Results Need Candidate Stories

### Material

- A new remote-sensing index improves wetland classification accuracy by 4%.
- It performs especially well during seasonal flooding.
- It fails in urban wetlands with mixed pixels.
- Field validation exists for two regions but not across climate zones.

### Candidate Stories

1. **Best story: seasonal visibility story.** The index works because it captures seasonal flooding signals that static wetland products miss.
2. **Alternative: method improvement story.** A modest classification gain becomes useful when the target class is seasonally dynamic.
3. **Risky story: global wetland monitoring breakthrough.** This overreaches because validation covers only two regions and mixed pixels remain a weakness.

### Calibrated Claim

The strongest supportable claim is not "a new global wetland product." It is:

> The index improves detection of seasonally flooded wetlands in validated regions, especially where existing products miss temporal water dynamics.

### Rewrite Target

Make the title, abstract, and first result emphasize seasonal detectability rather than generic accuracy gain.

## Example 4: Discussion That Overclaims

### Before

> These results demonstrate that the intervention improves patient outcomes and should be considered for clinical use.

### Evidence Actually Given

- Retrospective cohort.
- Biomarker response improved.
- No randomized trial.
- No long-term patient endpoint.

### Calibrated After

> These results support the biomarker as a candidate response indicator and justify prospective testing of whether the intervention improves patient outcomes. They do not yet establish clinical benefit.

### Why It Works

The revised claim preserves importance without pretending the evidence proves more than it does.
