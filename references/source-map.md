# Source Map

This file lists public sources that informed the skill. Use it when citing the basis for advice or expanding the corpus.

## Writing Guidance

- Joshua Schimel, *Writing Science: How to Write Papers That Get Cited and Proposals That Get Funded*, Oxford University Press, 2011/2012. Core lessons: successful science writing tells a story; story structure should shape papers, proposals, sections, paragraphs, sentences, and words; OCAR (Opening, Challenge, Action, Resolution) is a practical scaffold for finding the story. https://www.oupjapan.co.jp/en/products/detail/9759?language=en and https://schimelwritingscience.wordpress.com/2021/10/06/writing-science-getting-started-group-exercise/
- Brett Mensh and Konrad Kording, "Ten simple rules for structuring papers", PLOS Computational Biology, 2017. Core lessons: single central contribution, reader-first writing, C-C-C structure, complete abstract, gap-focused introduction, claim-bearing result sequence. https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1005619
- George D. Gopen and Judith A. Swan, "The Science of Scientific Writing", American Scientist, 1990. Core lessons: reader expectations, old-before-new information flow, stress position, subject as story owner. https://plantscience.psu.edu/research/labs/guiltinan/resources/readings-in-scientific-method-and-writing/the-science-of-scientific-writing/view
- George M. Whitesides, "Whitesides' Group: Writing a Paper", Advanced Materials, 2004. Core lessons: start with an outline, make figures and claims drive the paper, write to communicate the message rather than to record chronology. https://www.gmwgroup.harvard.edu/publications/whitesides-group-writing-paper
- Joshua R. Sanes, "Point of View: Tell me a story", eLife, 2019. Core lessons: scientific articles are constrained by truth and format, but benefit from explicit story construction. https://elifesciences.org/articles/50527
- John Swales's CARS model, summarized by Purdue OWL's "Creating a Research Space" handout. Core lessons: research introductions establish a territory, establish a niche, and occupy that niche with the present contribution. https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/graduate_writing/introduction_to_writing/documents/getting-started-with-grad-writing/cars-model-activity.pdf
- Randy Olson's ABT framework, summarized for scientific storytelling by MIT Communication Lab. Core lessons: "And, But, Therefore" creates concise narrative tension by moving from what is known, to the gap or conflict, to the attempted resolution. https://mitcommlab.mit.edu/cee/commkit/scientific-storytelling/
- Wayne C. Booth, Gregory G. Colomb, Joseph M. Williams, Joseph Bizup, and William T. FitzGerald, *The Craft of Research*, University of Chicago Press. Core lessons: move from topic to question to problem; build arguments from claims, reasons, evidence, warrants, qualifications, and responses to objections; answer "so what?" https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/C/bo215874008
- Stephen Toulmin's argument model, summarized by Purdue OWL. Core lessons: claims need grounds/evidence and warrants; qualifiers and rebuttals strengthen credibility by calibrating scope and acknowledging alternatives. https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/general_writing/academic_writing/historical_perspectives_on_argumentation/toulmin_argument.html
- George Heilmeier's Catechism, DARPA. Core lessons: a proposal-quality story answers no-jargon objective, current limits, novelty, who cares, risks, cost/time, and tests of success. https://www.darpa.mil/about/heilmeier-catechism
- Stephen B. Heard, *The Scientist's Guide to Writing*, Princeton University Press. Core lessons: clarity, deliberate writing practice, scientific-paper structure, revision, and finding/telling the story. https://books.google.com/books?vid=ISBN0691170215
- Margaret Cargill and Patrick O'Connor, *Writing Scientific Research Articles: Strategy and Steps*. Core lessons: results as the key story driver, turning data into knowledge, and writing each section strategically for peer-reviewed publication. https://discover.library.unt.edu/catalog/b4216372
- Gerald Graff and Cathy Birkenstein, *They Say/I Say: The Moves That Matter in Academic Writing*. Core lesson: position a new claim as a response to an existing scholarly conversation. https://books.google.com/books/about/They_Say_I_Say.html?id=gjuuKQEACAAJ
- David J. S. Montagnes, E. Ian Montagnes, and Zhou Yang, "Finding your scientific story by writing backwards", Marine Life Science & Technology, 2021/2022. Core lessons: begin from conclusions and work backwards through Results, Methods, Discussion, Introduction, Abstract, Title. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42995-021-00120-z
- University of Waterloo Writing and Communication Centre, "Engaging storytelling in science writing". Core lessons: narrative arc can map to academic argument; arrange points to build tension and land significance. https://uwaterloo.ca/writing-and-communication-centre/engaging-storytelling-science-writing
- Nature Masterclasses, "Narrative Tools for Researchers". Core lesson: narrative techniques can help natural-science researchers communicate to peers. https://www.nature.com/masterclasses/narrative-tools/52046744
- Nature Methods editorial, "Should scientists tell stories?", 2013. Core caution: story can guide figures and comprehension, but cherry-picking is a danger. https://www.nature.com/articles/nmeth.2726
- Nature Biomedical Engineering editorial, "Avoid hype", 2017. Core caution: factual accuracy, caveats, costs, endpoints, study design, correlation/causation, context, limitations, and previous research must not be obscured. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41551-017-0151-4
- EQUATOR Network, "How to write a great research paper using reporting guidelines". Core lesson: responsible research reporting requires complete and transparent reporting guidelines appropriate to study design. https://www.equator-network.org/toolkits/writing-research/
- Isabelle Boutron et al., "Reporting and Interpretation of Randomized Controlled Trials With Statistically Nonsignificant Results for Primary Outcomes", JAMA, 2010. Core caution: "spin" can distort presentation and interpretation, especially when conclusions overemphasize benefit despite nonsignificant primary outcomes. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20501928/

## Exemplar Papers

- J. D. Watson and F. H. C. Crick, "Molecular Structure of Nucleic Acids: A Structure for Deoxyribose Nucleic Acid", Nature, 1953. Pattern: compact model with vast implication. https://www.nature.com/articles/171737a0
- Douglas Hanahan and Robert A. Weinberg, "The Hallmarks of Cancer", Cell, 2000. Pattern: taxonomy that makes a sprawling field navigable. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10647931/
- Martin Jinek et al., "A programmable dual-RNA-guided DNA endonuclease in adaptive bacterial immunity", Science, 2012. Pattern: natural mechanism becomes programmable tool. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22745249/
- John Jumper et al., "Highly accurate protein structure prediction with AlphaFold", Nature, 2021. Pattern: a long-standing bottleneck is broken and validated at field scale. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03819-2

## Ecology Exemplar Papers

These papers provide the ecology domain calibration pack in `references/ecology-story-exemplars.md`; they calibrate local story judgment, not the boundary of `good-story`. Crossref cited-by counts were checked on 2026-06-02 and are approximate.

- Paine, R. T. (1966). "Food Web Complexity and Species Diversity", *The American Naturalist*. DOI: 10.1086/282400. Pattern: interaction structure and keystone logic. Crossref cited-by: about 3524. https://doi.org/10.1086/282400
- Odum, E. P. (1969). "The Strategy of Ecosystem Development", *Science*. DOI: 10.1126/science.164.3877.262. Pattern: conceptual synthesis that organizes succession. Crossref cited-by: about 3933. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.164.3877.262
- Tilman, D., Wedin, D., and Knops, J. (1996). "Productivity and sustainability influenced by biodiversity in grassland ecosystems", *Nature*. DOI: 10.1038/379718a0. Pattern: field experiment linking biodiversity to function. Crossref cited-by: about 2037. https://doi.org/10.1038/379718a0
- Hooper et al. (2005). "Effects of biodiversity on ecosystem functioning: a consensus of current knowledge", *Ecological Monographs*. DOI: 10.1890/04-0922. Pattern: consensus map of a contentious field. Crossref cited-by: about 6130. https://doi.org/10.1890/04-0922
- Estes et al. (2011). "Trophic Downgrading of Planet Earth", *Science*. DOI: 10.1126/science.1205106. Pattern: scattered trophic cascades become a planetary process. Crossref cited-by: about 3440. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1205106
- Cardinale et al. (2012). "Biodiversity loss and its impact on humanity", *Nature*. DOI: 10.1038/nature11148. Pattern: biodiversity mechanism linked to human consequence. Crossref cited-by: about 6034. https://doi.org/10.1038/nature11148
- Dirzo et al. (2014). "Defaunation in the Anthropocene", *Science*. DOI: 10.1126/science.1251817. Pattern: naming a hidden functional crisis. Crossref cited-by: about 3473. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1251817
- Newbold et al. (2015). "Global effects of land use on local terrestrial biodiversity", *Nature*. DOI: 10.1038/nature14324. Pattern: local biodiversity change scaled to global pressure. Crossref cited-by: about 3665. https://doi.org/10.1038/nature14324
- Diaz et al. (2019). "Pervasive human-driven decline of life on Earth points to the need for transformative change", *Science*. DOI: 10.1126/science.aax3100. Pattern: assessment-to-action synthesis. Crossref cited-by: about 2137. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aax3100

## Expansion Criteria

Add a source only if it contributes at least one reusable story move:

- A better way to find the central claim.
- A better way to sequence evidence.
- A better way to calibrate claims to evidence.
- A paper archetype that can help diagnose user materials.
- A caution against story-driven distortion.

## Publication-Environment Context

These sources inform the skill's caution that recent papers may be more explicit in framing because attention is scarcer and publication volume is larger, but that this does not automatically make them stronger scientific stories.

- Hanson et al., "The strain on scientific publishing", *Quantitative Science Studies*, 2024, summarized by NISO. Core lesson: indexed article volume grew sharply in recent years, increasing pressure on readers, reviewers, and quality signals. https://www.niso.org/niso-io/2024/09/more-more-more-strain-scientific-publishing
- National Science Board / NSF, *Science & Engineering Indicators*. Core lesson: global science and engineering publication output has expanded substantially across decades, with growth varying by country, region, and field. https://ncses.nsf.gov/indicators
- Park, Leahey, and Funk, "Papers and patents are becoming less disruptive over time", *Nature*, 2023. Core lesson: more scientific output does not necessarily mean more disruptive contribution; story evaluation must separate presentation polish from field-changing power. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-022-05543-x
