# Exemplar Paper Patterns

Use this reference to learn from memorable high-impact papers without copying their prose.

## Pattern A: The One-Page Thunderbolt

Example: Watson and Crick, "Molecular Structure of Nucleic Acids: A Structure for Deoxyribose Nucleic Acid" (Nature, 1953).

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: the field needs a structure that explains biological function.
- Turn: a model whose geometry implies a copying mechanism.
- Why memorable: the claim is compact, visual, and consequential; the implication is larger than the data presentation.
- Use when: the user's work has a simple model that explains multiple observations.
- Risk: elegance can hide missing evidence; name supporting evidence and historical dependencies honestly.

## Pattern B: The Taxonomy That Makes Chaos Navigable

Example: Hanahan and Weinberg, "The Hallmarks of Cancer" (Cell, 2000).

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: a sprawling literature lacks a portable organizing frame.
- Turn: a small set of principles makes diverse findings comparable.
- Why memorable: readers can reuse the categories as a mental map.
- Use when: the user's contribution is a synthesis, framework, typology, or conceptual model.
- Risk: categories can over-simplify; define boundaries and exceptions.

## Pattern C: The Bottleneck Is Broken

Example: Jumper et al., "Highly accurate protein structure prediction with AlphaFold" (Nature, 2021).

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: experimental structure determination is slow relative to sequence growth; prediction has been a decades-long hard problem.
- Turn: benchmarked performance reaches practical accuracy for many cases.
- Why memorable: the paper ties technical advance to a field-scale bottleneck and validates against an accepted challenge.
- Use when: the user's method changes scale, accuracy, cost, speed, or feasibility.
- Risk: method papers must prove reliability, limits, and use cases, not just novelty.

## Pattern D: Natural System Becomes Programmable Tool

Example: Jinek et al., "A programmable dual-RNA-guided DNA endonuclease in adaptive bacterial immunity" (Science, 2012).

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: a biological system is known, but its programmable potential is not yet operational.
- Turn: mechanistic dissection converts a natural process into a designable system.
- Why memorable: the story moves from mechanism to capability.
- Use when: the user's finding both explains a system and enables intervention.
- Risk: distinguish demonstrated capability from downstream applications.

## Pattern E: The Default Assumption Fails

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: the field assumes X is the main driver.
- Turn: a decisive comparison shows Y explains more.
- Why memorable: readers remember what they must stop saying.
- Use when: the user's data contain a clean contrast against a prevailing explanation.
- Risk: reviewers will demand the strongest alternative explanations; address them early.

## Pattern F: The Hidden Axis

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: variation looks noisy or context-specific.
- Turn: a latent variable, gradient, or dimension organizes the observations.
- Why memorable: complexity becomes legible.
- Use when: the user's analyses reveal structure in heterogeneity.
- Risk: axes require validation across datasets, not just attractive visualization.

## Pattern G: The Boundary Condition

Story pattern:

- Antagonist: a general rule is widely used but poorly scoped.
- Turn: systematic tests show where it holds and where it breaks.
- Why memorable: the paper saves readers from misuse.
- Use when: the user's evidence is careful, comparative, and perhaps less flashy than a new mechanism.
- Risk: the story must sell usefulness, not just caution.

## How to Apply These Patterns

Do not force the user's work into a famous-paper costume. Use the patterns as diagnostic mirrors:

1. Which antagonist fits the user's evidence?
2. Which turn is actually demonstrated?
3. What sentence would a reader repeat?
4. What would reviewers attack?
5. What narrower, truer story would survive that attack?
