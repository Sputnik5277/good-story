# Story Principles

Use this reference when turning scientific materials into a compelling, evidence-faithful story.

## Schimel's OCAR Arc

Joshua Schimel's central move in *Writing Science* is to treat scientific writing as story structure across scales: paper, proposal, section, paragraph, sentence, and word choice. Use OCAR as a first-pass scaffold:

- Opening: the larger problem, field setting, and reason the work matters to the widest honest audience.
- Challenge: the specific question, hypothesis, contradiction, bottleneck, or unknown that creates tension.
- Action: the 2-3 most important results or arguments that answer the challenge.
- Resolution: the central conclusion and take-home message; what the reader should remember and how it answers the challenge opened earlier.

Use OCAR to diagnose missing story parts:

- No Opening: technically accurate but nobody knows why it matters.
- No Challenge: topic overview without tension.
- No Action: claim without a belief-changing evidence path.
- No Resolution: data dump without a take-home change in understanding.

For papers and proposals, make the Introduction move from Opening to Challenge, the Results/Approach carry the Action, and the Discussion/Impact deliver the Resolution. For paragraphs, use the same arc in miniature when the paragraph does real argumentative work.

## ABT, CARS, and Toulmin in One Pass

Use these frameworks as different lenses on the same truthful argument:

- ABT: `And` establishes what is known; `But` names the gap or tension; `Therefore` gives the action or claim.
- CARS: establish territory, establish niche, occupy the niche.
- Toulmin: state claim, grounds, warrant, qualifier, and rebuttal.

Fast synthesis:

1. Write the ABT sentence.
2. Convert `And` into CARS territory.
3. Convert `But` into CARS niche.
4. Convert `Therefore` into the paper's central claim.
5. Test that claim with Toulmin: evidence, warrant, qualifier, rebuttal.

If the Toulmin test fails, revise the story. Do not hide the failure with better prose.

## 1. The Rule of One

A strong paper is built around one central contribution. Supporting results can be numerous, but they should all make the same final claim more believable. If two contributions compete, either find the bridge that makes them one contribution or split the story.

Operational questions:

- What is the one thing the field can now say, do, or believe because of this work?
- Which result would make the paper collapse if removed?
- Which details are true but not story-bearing?

## 2. Context-Content-Conclusion at Every Scale

Use C-C-C recursively:

- Paper: Introduction gives context, Results provide content, Discussion delivers conclusion.
- Abstract: broad context, specific gap, approach/results, conclusion/implication.
- Paragraph: first sentence orients, middle advances, final sentence lands.
- Figure: title states the conclusion, panels provide the evidence, legend clarifies the test.

If a reader asks "Why am I being told this?", context is missing. If they ask "So what?", conclusion is missing.

## 3. Reader Expectation Is Architecture

Readers are not empty containers; they constantly predict where information is going. Put old/contextual information before new information. Put the sentence's stress-worthy point near the end. Keep the grammatical subject aligned with the thing whose story is being told.

Revision move:

1. Underline the old/context material in each sentence.
2. Underline the new/stress material.
3. Move the old material left and the new material right.
4. Make adjacent sentences pass the baton: the end of one sentence should prepare the beginning of the next.

## 4. The Antagonist Must Be Real

"Little is known" is rarely enough. A strong antagonist is a reason the work was hard or necessary:

- A field belief that blocks a new interpretation.
- A contradiction between observations.
- A technical bottleneck.
- A scale mismatch.
- A missing mechanism.
- A practical decision that existing knowledge cannot support.

The antagonist should make the reader want the method or experiment before it appears.

## 5. The Turn Must Change Belief

The turn is the result, comparison, model, or synthesis after which the reader cannot return to the old view unchanged. It is often not the biggest dataset; it is the most belief-moving test.

Good turns do one of these:

- Convert correlation into mechanism.
- Convert a special case into a general principle.
- Convert a method from possible to useful.
- Resolve a contradiction.
- Reveal that the important variable was not the one the field assumed.

## 6. Evidence Ladder Before Chronology

Do not tell the story in lab-notebook order unless the discovery process itself is the contribution. Order results by logical dependence:

1. Establish the system/problem.
2. Validate the measurement or model.
3. Show the central effect.
4. Rule out key alternatives.
5. Extend or generalize.
6. Explain mechanism or consequence.

Each figure should answer the question created by the previous figure.

## 7. Breadth-Narrow-Breadth

High-impact scientific stories often move:

1. Broad field problem.
2. Narrow unresolved gap.
3. Specific evidence.
4. Broader consequence.

The narrow part protects credibility; the broad part creates significance.

## 8. Story Integrity

Storytelling in science is constrained by truth.

- Mark speculation as speculation.
- Do not hide negative results that affect the claim.
- Do not imply causality from association.
- Do not turn limitations into vague future work; use them to define scope.
- Preserve uncertainty when uncertainty is scientifically important.

The best story is not the prettiest possible story; it is the strongest honest story.

## 9. Useful Story Archetypes

- Mechanism revealed: "We knew X happened; now we know how."
- Bottleneck broken: "The field could not do X at scale/accuracy/speed; now it can."
- Dogma revised: "The accepted model misses Y."
- Reconciliation: "Conflicting findings become coherent under Z."
- Map made navigable: "A messy space can be organized by a small set of principles."
- Tool enables biology: "A method matters because it unlocks a question that was previously inaccessible."
- Boundary condition: "A celebrated rule works, but only under these conditions."

## 10. Good-Story Review Rubric

Score each from 0-3:

- Centrality: one memorable contribution.
- Stakes: a real reason readers should care.
- Tension: an obstacle or unresolved contradiction.
- Evidence: sufficient support for the claim.
- Logic: results ordered by inference, not chronology.
- Surprise: at least one belief-moving turn.
- Scope: claims calibrated to evidence.
- Portability: easy to retell outside the immediate subfield.

Total:

- 20-24: strong story; polish delivery.
- 14-19: viable story; strengthen gap, turn, or evidence order.
- 8-13: scattered material; choose a central contribution.
- 0-7: not yet a story; return to claims and evidence.
