# Overclaim Calibration

Use this reference when the story is strong but may outrun the evidence. The goal is not to weaken a paper; the goal is to make its strongest defensible claim survive expert review.

## Claim Ladder

Move up this ladder only when the evidence supports it:

1. Observation: "We observed X."
2. Association: "X is associated with Y."
3. Prediction: "X predicts Y under these conditions."
4. Experimental effect: "Manipulating X changed Y."
5. Mechanism: "X changes Y through Z."
6. Generality: "This pattern holds across systems or contexts."
7. Application: "This can guide management, policy, design, or intervention."

If the data support level 3, do not write a level-5 story. If the data support one system, do not write a level-6 story without qualifiers.

## Common Overclaim Moves

| Tempting phrase | Risk | Safer move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "proves" | Usually too strong | "supports", "is consistent with", "provides evidence for" |
| "drives" | Implies causality | Use only with causal design or strong mechanistic evidence |
| "mechanism" | Implies process evidence | Use "possible mechanism" or "candidate pathway" if indirect |
| "universal" | Implies broad scope | State the tested systems and conditions |
| "transformative" | Hype unless field consequences are demonstrated | State the specific change in what readers can do or infer |
| "first" | Priority claim is fragile | Use only after literature check; otherwise "to our knowledge" |
| "explains" | Can overstate completeness | Use "helps explain" or "accounts for part of" |
| "reveals" | Can imply direct discovery | Use when evidence is direct; otherwise "suggests" |

## Calibration Questions

Before accepting a story, ask:

- What is the exact strongest claim?
- What evidence directly supports it?
- What evidence is only suggestive?
- What alternative explanation remains plausible?
- What result is negative, null, or limiting?
- What system, scale, taxon, region, time window, or method bounds the claim?
- What would a skeptical reviewer say is missing?
- What word needs a qualifier?

## Reviewer-Safe Rewrites

Use this pattern:

`Strong claim + scope + evidence basis + remaining uncertainty`

Examples:

- Too strong: "Biodiversity loss reduces ecosystem sustainability."
- Calibrated: "In this grassland experiment, lower plant diversity reduced productivity and weakened nitrogen retention, supporting the view that biodiversity can contribute to ecosystem sustainability."

- Too strong: "Land use causes global biodiversity collapse."
- Calibrated: "Across the sampled terrestrial assemblages, land-use intensity was associated with substantial losses of local biodiversity, indicating a major pressure on biodiversity under the studied conditions."

- Too strong: "This mechanism explains community stability."
- Calibrated: "These results support one mechanism by which diversity can stabilize community function, while leaving open whether the same pathway dominates in other systems."

## When To Keep A Bold Claim

Do not overcorrect into timid prose. Keep the bold claim when:

- The study design directly tests the claim.
- Key alternatives are addressed.
- The claim is scoped to the evidence.
- The implication follows from the result rather than from hope.
- The paper states uncertainty without burying the contribution.

The best calibrated claim is not smaller than necessary; it is exactly as large as the evidence allows.
